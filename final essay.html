Introduction Paragraph

The two passages present conflicting views on the topic of [topic of both articles]. In the first passage, [Author 1's Last Name] argues that [explain Author 1's main claim in your own words]. Conversely, in the second passage, [Author 2's Last Name] claims that [explain Author 2's main claim in your own words]. After analyzing both arguments, it is clear that [Author's Last Name] presents the more convincing and well-supported case by effectively using [list the 2-3 key types of evidence you will discuss].

Body Paragraph #1: Analyze Strong Evidence

First, [Stronger Author's Last Name] effectively builds their argument by using [type of evidence, e.g., a government study]. The author states, ["quote or paraphrase the specific piece of evidence"]. This evidence is highly convincing because [explain *why* this evidence is powerful].

Body Paragraph #2: Analyze More Strong Evidence

Furthermore, [Stronger Author's Last Name] strengthens their position with [another type of evidence]. For example, the author points out that ["quote or paraphrase the second piece of evidence"]. This is a logical and persuasive point because [explain *why* this reasoning is effective].

Body Paragraph #3: Analyze the Weaker Argument

In contrast, the argument presented by [Weaker Author's Last Name] is not as well-supported. A key weakness is the author's reliance on [identify a weakness, e.g., unsupported claims]. For instance, the author claims that ["quote or paraphrase a weak piece of evidence"]. This argument is unconvincing because [explain *why* it is weak].

Conclusion Paragraph

In conclusion, while both authors address the topic of [topic of both articles], [Stronger Author's Last Name] presents a far more compelling argument. By skillfully using [restate the types of strong evidence], the author builds a logical and evidence-based case that is ultimately more persuasive than the weakly supported claims made by [Weaker Author's Last Name].

Statistical Data

Using numbers, percentages, or data from research. Numbers feel like hard facts, so they can be very convincing.

Example: "A new study shows that 78% of people want more parks in their city."

Expert Testimony

Quoting an expert on the subject. This makes the argument seem more credible because it's supported by a professional.

Example: "Dr. Aliza Carter, a leading climate scientist, says that sea levels are rising faster than ever."

Logical Reasoning

Connecting ideas in a way that makes sense (like cause-and-effect). It shows the author has thought through their position.

Example: "If employees are less stressed, they will be more productive. Therefore, a four-day work week will boost company output."

Anecdotal Evidence

Telling a short, relevant story to make a point more relatable and human. It connects with the reader's experience.

Example: "Last year, my own company switched to a new software, and our efficiency immediately improved by 15%."

Unsupported Claims

When an author makes a statement that something is a fact but offers zero proof. It's an opinion pretending to be a fact.

Example: "It's obvious that teens don't read books anymore."

Overgeneralization

Making a huge conclusion based on only one or two examples. A small case is used to make a rule for a large group.

Example: "I saw a tourist drop some trash, which proves that all tourists are messy."

Emotional Appeal

Trying to win by making the reader feel sad, angry, or happy, instead of using facts. It distracts from the real issue.

Example: "Think of the sad, lonely puppies. You must donate to our animal shelter right now."

False Cause

Incorrectly assuming that because two things happened together, one must have caused the other. It's a classic mix-up.

Example: "Our city's baseball team started winning after the new stadium was built, so the stadium caused the wins."
